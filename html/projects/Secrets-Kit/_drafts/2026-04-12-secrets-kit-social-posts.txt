Introducing Secrets Kit v0.9.0.

Most local environments are full of secrets scattered across .env files, shell startup scripts, copied commands, and project directories. Somehow they still find their way into your GitHub repo... or worse, you wake up to a $372k OpenAI API bill.

Secrets Kit is a macOS CLI that pulls those values out of plain text and into the system Keychain, keeps non-sensitive metadata in a local registry, and only exports environment variables when a runtime actually needs them.

It is not a vault service and it does not claim perfect security.

It is a practical step toward fixing secret sprawl and improving local security hygiene without changing how real workflows operate.

Useful for agent runtimes, local UIs, shell workflows, and development stacks that still rely on environment variables but should not rely on secrets living all over the filesystem.

Title

Introducing Secrets Kit

Subtitle

A practical way to stop leaking secrets all over your local machine

⸻

Body

Most local environments are a mess and I've never liked the idea of secrets just sitting around in plain text, but like most people, I tolerated it longer than I should have.

Not in an obvious way - everything works. Your scripts run, your apps start, your APIs respond. But underneath that, there's usually a trail of secrets scattered everywhere: .env files, shell startup configs, copied commands sitting in history, random project directories you forgot about.

And somehow, sooner or later, one of those ends up where it shouldn't. A Git commit. A shared repo. Or worse, a bill you weren't expecting.

Secrets Kit came out of dealing with that problem over and over again.

The idea is simple: stop leaving sensitive values in plain text, but don't make your workflow worse in the process.

Secrets Kit stores the actual secret values in the macOS login Keychain, keeps lightweight metadata in a local registry, and only exposes environment variables when something actually needs them. Your tools still get what they expect, but your secrets aren't just sitting around on disk waiting to be leaked.

It's not a vault service. It's not trying to be "perfect security." It's just a practical step toward better local hygiene.

That makes it useful anywhere you're still relying on environment variables - which is basically everywhere: local development, agent runtimes, shell workflows, automation scripts, and the glue code that holds all of that together.

It's one of those problems that doesn't look like a big deal until it is. This is just a cleaner way to handle it before it becomes one.
